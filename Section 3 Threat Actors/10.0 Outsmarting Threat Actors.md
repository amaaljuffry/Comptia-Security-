
### **1. Introduction**

Attackers are constantly seeking ways to exploit organizations ‚Äî whether to steal **sensitive data**, **digital assets**, or **resources**.  
To effectively defend against them, cybersecurity teams must **move from reactive to proactive defense** by studying attackers‚Äô methods and disrupting their operations.

A key proactive approach is to use **Deception and Disruption Technologies** ‚Äî tools designed to **mislead, detect, and counter** threat actors by studying their **Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs)**.

---

### **2. Understanding TTPs**

**Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs)** represent the specific **patterns of behavior** and **methods** used by threat actors during attacks.

- **Tactics:** The _why_ ‚Äî the attacker‚Äôs goals or objectives.
- **Techniques:** The _how_ ‚Äî the general methods used to achieve the goal.
- **Procedures:** The _details_ ‚Äî specific implementations or steps taken.

By studying TTPs, cybersecurity professionals can:

- Detect new and emerging threats.
- Develop targeted defenses.
- Understand attacker motivations and strategies.

---

### **3. Deception & Disruption Technologies**
These technologies **mislead**, **confuse**, and **divert** attackers ‚Äî while collecting intelligence and buying defenders time to respond.

There are **four main deception tools** and several **supporting disruption strategies**.

### **1. Honeypots**

- **Definition:** A _decoy system_ designed to attract attackers and record their activities.
- **Purpose:**
    - Gather intelligence on attacker methods (TTPs).
    - Detect insider threats or unauthorized access.
    - Study malware behavior safely.
- **Best practice:**
    - Place honeypots in a **screened subnet or isolated network segment**.
    - Log all attacker interactions for later analysis.
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **Risk:** If misconfigured, attackers can learn details about your real systems.
### **2. Honeynets**

- **Definition:** A _network of honeypots_ simulating a full enterprise environment (servers, routers, switches).
- **Purpose:**
    - Study complex attack patterns.
    - Identify large-scale attack trends and vulnerabilities.
- **Use:**
    - Typically deployed by **large organizations or research institutions**.
    - Helps reveal **attack patterns** across multiple systems.
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **Risk:** Attackers may use the honeynet to study your defenses if not properly isolated.

### **3. Honeyfiles**

- **Definition:** A _fake file_ designed to look valuable (e.g., ‚ÄúPayroll2025.xlsx‚Äù) but actually acts as a **trap**.
- **Features:**
    - Contains **fake or watermarked data**.
    - Alerts security teams when accessed.
    - May include code to **enumerate the attacker‚Äôs network**.
- **Common formats:**
    - Documents, spreadsheets, images, databases, executables.
- **Purpose:** Detect intrusions and trace stolen data.

### **4. Honeytokens**

- **Definition:** A _fake data object_ or _digital resource_ that has **no legitimate use**, but triggers alerts when accessed.
- **Examples:**
    - Fake admin user account.
    - Dummy database entry.
    - Bogus API key or URL.
- **Purpose:**
    - Detect insider threats or external data breaches.
    - Identify unauthorized access early.
- **Example:**  
    A fake account ‚ÄúAdmin_Test‚Äù ‚Äî if anyone tries to log in, an alert is triggered.

## ‚öôÔ∏è **Disruption Technologies & Strategies**

Beyond deception, disruption techniques **waste attackers‚Äô time**, **create confusion**, and **hide real systems**.

### **1. Bogus DNS Entries**
- Create **fake DNS records** to mislead attackers into non-existent domains
- **Benefit:** Wastes attacker resources and alerts defenders of scanning attempts.
### **2. Decoy Directories**
- Place **fake folders and files** on systems.
- **Purpose:** When accessed, trigger alerts and mislead attackers into thinking they‚Äôve succeeded.
### **3. Dynamic Page Generation**
- Use web servers that display **changing or randomized content**.
- **Purpose:** Confuse automated bots, scrapers, or reconnaissance tools.
### **4. Port Triggering to Hide Services**
- **Technique:** Keep service ports closed until a specific outbound traffic pattern is detected.
- **Purpose:** Prevent attackers from discovering active services through scans.
- **Benefit:** Legitimate users can still access services securely 
### **5. Spoofing Fake Telemetry Data**
- Send **false system or network information** when scans are detected.
- **Purpose:** Mislead attackers about your network‚Äôs layout and operating systems.
- **Example:** System pretends to run _Windows 11_ instead of _macOS_ to misdirect exploits.
